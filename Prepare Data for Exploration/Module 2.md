## Key Takeaways (Unbiased and objective data)

- **Bias**: a preference in favor of or against a person, group of people, or thing. It is important to recognize our own biases to manage them effectively.

- **Data Bias**: a type of error that systematically skews results in a certain direction. Examples include under-representation of certain populations in research, which can lead to significant consequences, such as in healthcare.

- **Implications of Bias**
	- Bias can influence various sectors, including business and healthcare, leading to unfair outcomes.
	
	- As data analysts, it is crucial to consider bias from data collection to presentation, as it can have serious implications on conclusions drawn from the data.

- **Sampling Bias**: when a sample isn't representative of the population as a whole.

- **Unbiased sampling**: when a sample is representative of the population being measured.

- **Avoiding Sampling Bias**
	- Random sampling ensures that all parts of the population have an equal chance of being included. An example illustrates how surveying a non-representative group can lead to incorrect conclusions.
	
	- Visualizations, such as bar charts, can help identify misalignments in sample representation. Comparing visual data can reveal biases and enhance understanding of the sample's credibility.

- **Types of Data Bias**
	- **Observer Bias**: This occurs when different observers perceive the same data differently, leading to inconsistent results. For example, two scientists may interpret the same microscopic image in various ways.
	
	- **Interpretation Bias**: This is the tendency to interpret ambiguous information based on personal experiences or emotions. For instance, a voicemail from a boss may be perceived as angry by one person and calm by another, affecting data interpretation.
	
	- **Confirmation Bias**: This bias involves favoring information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. It can lead to skewed data analysis as individuals may only seek out data that supports their views.

## Key Takeaways (Achieve data credibility)

- Identifying Good Data Sources
	- **Reliable**: Good data sources provide accurate, complete, and unbiased information that has been vetted.
	
	- **Original**: Validate data with the original source to ensure its authenticity.
	
	- **Comprehensive**: Data should contain all critical information necessary for analysis.
	
	- **Current**: Use data that is relevant and up-to-date to ensure its usefulness.
	
	- **Cited**: Credibility is enhanced by citing the source of the data, including who created it and when it was last updated.

- Identifying Bad Data Sources
	- Bad data sources are characterized by **a lack of reliability, originality, comprehensiveness, currency, and citation.**
	- Examples include misleading visualizations, such as bar graphs with manipulated y-axis starting points.

- Understanding the ROCCC Framework
	- ROCCC stands for Reliable, Original, Comprehensive, Current, and Cited data.
	- If data does not meet these criteria, it is considered bad data that can lead to incorrect conclusions and poor decision-making.

- Importance of Good Data
	- Good data is essential for accurate analysis and decision-making in data analytics.
	- Reliable sources include vetted public datasets, academic papers, and governmental agency data, which help avoid the pitfalls of bad data.

## Key Takeaways (Data ethics and privacy)

- **Understanding Ethics**
	- **Ethics**: well-founded standards of right and wrong that prescribe what humans ought to do, usually in terms of rights, obligations, benefits to society, fairness, or specific virtues.
	
	- **Data ethics**: well-founded standards of right and wrong that dictate how data is collected, shared, and used.
	
	- **Aspects of data ethics**
		- **Ownership**: Individuals own the raw data they provide and they have primary control over its usage, how it's processed, how it's shared.
		
		- **Transaction transparency**: All data-processing activities and algorithms should be completely explainable and understood by the individual who provides their data.
		
		- **Consent**: An individual's right to know explicit details about how and why their data will be used before agreeing to provide it.
		
		- **Currency**: Individuals should be aware of financial transactions resulting from the use of their personal data and the scale of these transactions.
		
		- **Privacy**: Preserving a data subject's information and activity any time a data transaction occurs.
		
		- **Openness**: Free access, usage, and sharing of data. Standards for open data include availability in modifiable formats, terms allowing reuse and redistribution, and ensuring universal participation without discrimination.
			- **Data interoperability**: the ability of data systems and services to openly connect and share data.
