## Key Takeaways (Understanding the power of data)

- **Understanding Data and Decision-Making**
	- Data is defined as a collection of facts that can reveal patterns and insights through analysis.
	- Data analysis aids in making informed decisions, as seen in everyday examples like searching for restaurants based on ratings.

- **Data-inspired decision-making**: explores different data sources to find out what they have in common.

- An **algorithm** is a process or set of rules to be followed for a specific task.

- **Data-Driven vs. Data-Inspired Decisions** | [[Case Study Data-driven & Data-inspired decision making|Case Study]]
	- Data-driven decisions rely heavily on data analysis, while data-inspired decisions explore various data sources for commonalities.
	- Real-life applications include Google's use of data to improve energy efficiency in data centers and enhance hiring processes through algorithms.

- **Transforming Data into Knowledge**
	- Data alone has limited value; it must be interpreted to become meaningful information.
	- Context is crucial for understanding data, as demonstrated by comparing Michael Phelps' race times to determine his performance in competitions.

- **A data analysis triumph**: When data is used strategically, businesses can transform and grow their revenue. [[Case Study Data Trials and Triumphs | Case Study]]

- **Data analysts should balance data-driven and data-inspired approaches**, leveraging both logic and creativity in their analyses to ensure accurate interpretations and effective decision-making.

- **Quantitative data**: specific and objective measures of numerical facts. Involves numerical facts and objective measures, answering questions like "how many" or "how often." It provides certainty and accuracy, often visualized through charts or graphs.

- **Qualitative data**: subjective or explanatory measures of qualities and characteristics. Describes subjective qualities and characteristics, helping to answer "why" questions. It provides context to the numbers, enhancing understanding of underlying issues.

- **Application in Business**
	- **Customer Reviews Example**: A local ice cream shop uses online reviews to gather insights. Quantitative data reveals the number of negative reviews, while qualitative data uncovers reasons for customer dissatisfaction, such as running out of popular flavors.
	- **Decision-Making**: Combining both data types allows businesses to identify problems and implement effective solutions, improving customer satisfaction and overall performance.

## Key Takeaways (Follow the evidence)

- **Report**: static collection of data given to stakeholders periodically. 
	**Pros:**
	- Reports are great for giving snapshots of high level historical data for an organization.
	- Easy to design.
	- Pre-cleaned and sorted data.
	**Cons**:
	- Continual maintenance.
	- Less visually appealing.
	- Static.

- **Dashboard**: monitors live, incoming data. A dashboard would be useful for monitoring data as it becomes available. [[Design compelling dashboards]]
	**Pros**:
	- Dynamic, automatic, and interactive.
	- More stakeholder access.
	- Low maintenance.
	**Cons**:
	- Labor-intensive design.
	- Can be confusing.
	- Potentially uncleaned data.
	
	Often, businesses will tailor a dashboard for a specific purpose. [[Design compelling dashboards#The three most common categories are|The three most common categories are]]:
	- **Strategic**: focuses on long term goals and strategies at the highest level of metrics
	- **Operational:** short-term performance tracking and intermediate goals
	- **Analytical:** consists of the datasets and the mathematics used in these sets

- **Pivot table**: A data summarization tool that is used in data processing. Pivot tables are use to summarize, sort, reorganize, group, count, total or average data stored in a database.

- **Metric**: single, quantifiable type of data that can be used for measurement. Metrics can ben used to help calculate customer retention rates, or company’s ability to keep its customers overtime.

- **Metric goal**: a measurable goal set by a company and evaluated by metrics.

- **Tools for visualizing data**:
	**Spreadsheets**
	- Google Sheets and Microsoft Excel are commonly used for creating static charts and graphs, offering basic data visualization capabilities.
	- They allow for data cleaning, sorting, and filtering, and are integrated with other applications for seamless reporting and presentations.

	**Tableau**
	- Tableau is designed for creating interactive visualizations and dashboards, supporting a wide range of data sources and advanced analytics.
	- It is particularly effective for large datasets, though it requires time to learn effectively.

## Key Takeaways (Connect the dots)

- **Mathematical Thinking**
	- It involves breaking down problems logically to identify patterns in data.
	- This approach helps in selecting the most appropriate tools for analysis.

- **Types of Data** [[Big and small data]]
	- Small data consists of specific metrics over a short time, useful for daily decisions.
	- Big data encompasses larger datasets over extended periods, often requiring tools like SQL for analysis.

- **Application Example**
	- A hospital can use mathematical thinking to optimize bed usage by analyzing metrics like bed occupancy rates.
	- By identifying patterns, the hospital can make informed decisions, such as reducing the number of unused beds to save resources.
